About my research

My primary research interests lie in language documentation and the semantics of under-studied languages. Since 2013, my research has focused on the Zapotec languages of Oaxaca, Mexico. I also collaborate with international teams of linguists, historians, and language activists to support community-led projects aimed at language revalorization and youth empowerment, drawing on digital scholarship to improve access and communication within the transnational Zapotec community.

Documentation & description of Tlacolula Valley Zapotec

My current research focus is the expression of temporal, aspectual, and modal (``TAM'') semantics in Zapotec languages. My PhD Qualifying Paper investigated Tlacochahuaya Zapotec imperfectives. I presented on the semantic distribution of Tlacochahuaya Zapotec r- at SSILA 2020, showing that although this TAM prefix is typically described as a ``habitual'' it actually has a much broader use in natural speech, and at TLS 2020 I discussed how these findings fit into our understanding of imperfectives in Central Zapotec more broadly.

My descriptive research is closely intertwined with language documentation. I archive my data on a rolling basis in a collection at the Archive of the Indigenous Languages of Latin America. I am also a co-author on a Talking Dictionary for Tlacochahuaya Zapotec, and I have collaborated on Talking Dictionaries for San Lucas Quiaviní Zapotec and Teotitlán del Valle Zapotec. These multimedia dictionaries include audio recordings, pictures, videos, and embedded tweets, and every dictionary is coauthored with Zapotec collaborators.

I'm supported by a Graduate Research Fellowship from the NSF and the Harrington Doctoral Fellowship at UT Austin. My research on Tlacochahuaya Zapotec has been supported at various times by the National Science Foundation, the Jacobs Research Funds, and the Pan American Round Tables of Texas, as well as internal funding sources at Haverford College and UT Austin.

Ticha & Colonial Zapotec linguistics

I am a co-author of Ticha, a digital text explorer for Colonial Zapotec built by a team of linguists, historians, activists, and students, including both Zapotec and non-Indigenous scholars. As detailed in our 2020 article in Digital Humanities Quarterly, the Ticha Project combines digital scholarship, a philosophy of post-custodial archiving, and research practices embedded in community engagement to create rich, accessible resources on Colonial Zapotec.

In conjunction with an ACLS-funded project led by Brook Danielle Lillehaugen and other members of the Ticha team, I co-edited Caseidyneën Saën, a volume of educational materials on Colonial Zapotec which draw on the resources on Ticha. Using lessons from Caseidyneën Saën, our Zapotec collaborators led a series of workshops for Zapotec community members in Oaxaca and Oaxacalifornia. We presented on this process at ICLDC 2021.

In parallel with my work for Ticha, I have also studied the grammar of Colonial Valley Zapotec, including conjunction and resumptive pronouns in relative clauses. This philological perspective continues to inform my research on Zapotec languages as they are spoken today. As part of my early graduate fieldwork, I re-elicited a modern Tlacochahuaya Zapotec translation of a Colonial Valley Zapotec will.

Other projects

In the summer of 2019, I worked with J. Ryan Sullivant to develop a workflow to transform crowdsourced transcriptions of Kathryn Josserand's Mixtec syntax surveys into machine- and human-readable corpora. The resulting documents were publicly archived a the Archive of the Indigenous Languages of Latin America, and the process is documented in the project's whitepaper.

I served as a volunteer reader on the Voces del Valle project, an initiative to support Zapotec speakers in writing their language on social media, and participated on a panel on Twitter and Oaxaca languages at COLOV 2018. (For background, see Lillehaugen 2016.)

In 2015 and 2016, I participated in Linguistics Field Schools led by Brook Danielle Lillehaugen, K. David Harrison, Ted Ferald (part of the NSF REU program). These programs included leading Zapotec language arts workshops for high school students in Tlacolula de Matamoros, Oaxaca, and supporting field methods education for US undergraduate students.
